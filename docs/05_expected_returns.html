6. Expected returns: what the portfolio should earn from here

A portfolio can be well-constructed around genuine return sources and still disappoint if those return sources are expensively priced at the point of entry. Knowing what a portfolio holds is not the same as knowing what it should earn. The two questions require different tools, and the second is where investment process most often becomes imprecise.

The purpose of this section in the framework is specific. It provides the forward-looking standard against which the strategic core from section 7 should be evaluated — is the broad market exposure in the core priced to earn a return commensurate with its risk? It provides the arithmetic for comparing active additions against that baseline. And it establishes the regime conditioning that determines when the answers to both questions change.

The arithmetic identity

The most useful starting point for equity return estimation is a decomposition that forces the investor to state which element of return is actually being relied upon. Long-run equity return can be approximated as:

\[\text{Return} \approx \text{Starting yield} + \text{Real earnings growth} + \text{Inflation} + \text{Change in valuation multiple}\]

This is the Arnott and Bernstein decomposition (Arnott & Bernstein, 2002). Each component is observable or estimable independently. Starting yield is the current dividend yield. Real earnings growth per share is estimable from long-run productivity and demographic trends. Inflation is the expected price level change. The multiple change is the residual — the repricing contribution that cannot be known in advance but can be bounded by asking what the starting valuation implies about its likely direction.

The value of this decomposition is that it prevents a return forecast from being dominated by an assumption that was never stated. The most common hidden assumption is that the valuation multiple will remain stable or continue expanding. If the starting CAPE is 30x, the current dividend yield is 1.5 percent, and long-run real earnings growth is 1.5 percent, the arithmetic available from those two observable components is approximately 3 percent in real terms before any multiple change. If the investor’s return assumption is 7 percent real, the remaining 4 percent must come from a valuation-driven repricing of the market upward — an assumption that should be stated and defended rather than embedded silently.

The chart below applies this decomposition to four major historical regimes for the S&P 500. In 1982–1999 the exceptional returns were driven primarily by multiple expansion as the CAPE rose from approximately 8x to 44x — a repricing that was by definition unrepeatable from the conditions it produced. In 1966–1981 multiple contraction and inflation erosion dominated, producing near-zero real returns despite positive earnings growth. Reading these regimes backward as an unconditional average, and then applying that average as a forward forecast, imports all their favorable and unfavorable conditions into the projection without examination.

Starting valuation and the equity return anchor

The empirical case for starting valuation as a predictor of long-run equity returns is among the strongest in empirical finance. Campbell and Shiller showed that the cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings ratio — CAPE, defined as the current price divided by the ten-year average of real earnings — explains a substantial fraction of the variance in subsequent ten-year real equity returns (Campbell & Shiller, 1998). The relationship is not useful for short-run timing: at one- or two-year horizons CAPE has little predictive power. At seven to ten year horizons the relationship has been persistent across multiple markets and long data samples.

The scatter below shows this for the S&P 500 across the full sample from 1881 to 2015. The downward-sloping fit confirms the valuation logic. Starting from a CAPE of 44x in January 2000 was followed by a negative real return over the subsequent decade. Starting from a CAPE of 8x in January 1982 was followed by exceptional returns. The wide dispersion around the fit confirms the timing limitation: CAPE is an anchor for long-horizon expected return, not a short-run signal.

The mechanism is the arithmetic identity: when the CAPE is elevated, the yield component is compressed and the multiple must stay elevated or expand further for the historical average return to be achieved. At CAPE levels above 25x — where the U.S. market has spent much of the period since 2015 — the arithmetic requires either above-trend earnings growth, continued multiple expansion, or the investor accepting a lower forward return than history suggests.

Starting yield and the fixed-income anchor

For high-quality fixed-income instruments, starting yield is the most informative single number available for medium- to long-horizon return estimation. If a bond is held to maturity and does not default, the total nominal return equals the yield at purchase. At shorter horizons mark-to-market returns deviate as rates move, but the starting yield exerts a gravitational pull that strengthens as the holding period extends. Ilmanen documents this empirically: the correlation between starting yield and subsequent five- to ten-year total return for investment-grade bonds is high enough that starting yield functions as a reasonable central estimate for medium-horizon return planning (Ilmanen, 2012).

The chart below shows this for the 10-year U.S. Treasury since 1960. The scatter on the left confirms the strong positive relationship. The right panel shows the yield history, making visible the two key distortions: the 1981 peak at approximately 16 percent, which preceded exceptional bond returns as yields fell for the following four decades; and the 2020 trough at approximately 0.5 percent, which implied near-zero or negative real forward returns from that starting point.

An investor who allocated to ten-year Treasuries in mid-2020 on the basis of the prior decade’s realized bond returns was conflating one-time capital gains from yield compression with repeatable carry. When yields subsequently rose sharply in 2022, long-duration bonds fell approximately 30–35 percent — a loss of equity-like magnitude from an instrument classified as safe. The classification was correct about credit quality. It was incorrect about interest rate risk in a regime where the starting yield had no room to continue falling.

How macro regimes change the arithmetic

Starting conditions provide the raw material for a return estimate. The macro regime determines whether that estimate is realized. The same CAPE of 20x produces different outcomes in a disinflationary growth environment, a stagflationary environment, and a credit-stress environment — even if the starting number is identical.

Cochrane’s work on discount rate variation is the theoretical anchor here: most of the time-series variation in aggregate equity returns is explained by variation in discount rates rather than by variation in expected cash flow growth (Cochrane, 2011). A change in the inflation and policy regime changes the discount rate applied to every future cash flow in the portfolio simultaneously — which is why regime transitions tend to dominate medium-horizon realized returns.

The four regimes in the decomposition chart illustrate this. The 1966–1981 inflationary regime destroyed real returns on long-duration assets despite positive earnings growth. The 1982–2000 disinflationary expansion rewarded both equities and bonds through falling discount rates and strong earnings growth. The 2000–2009 period saw the unwinding of the 1990s valuation excess compounded by a leverage-driven credit crisis. The 2010–2021 period of quantitative easing and zero real rates sustained multiple expansion across nearly all asset classes while simultaneously compressing future expected returns from the higher prices those conditions produced.

For a systematic multi-asset portfolio that holds equity futures, bond futures, gold, and silver — as the strategy connected to this framework does — regime awareness is directly operational. Equity futures expose the portfolio to discount rate changes as well as earnings growth. Bond futures are sensitive to the inflation and policy stance. Gold and silver behave differently across regimes than financial assets, providing exposure that is most valuable when the core financial asset premia are compressed. A return estimate that does not condition on which regime the portfolio is entering will systematically overestimate return in adverse regimes and underestimate it in favorable ones.

Working in ranges

Any honest return estimate must acknowledge substantial uncertainty. The practical response is to construct a scenario range that identifies which assumption is doing most of the work in the central estimate, rather than presenting a single number that obscures the range.

In equities the three drivers that most commonly separate the base case from the adverse case are the path of real earnings growth, the level at which the valuation multiple stabilizes, and the trajectory of real interest rates. In fixed income the equivalent is the path of inflation and the level at which policy rates settle. For each scenario the arithmetic identity provides the structure: return is yield plus growth plus multiple change, and the case for each scenario is a statement about what values those components are assumed to take.

The hidden stability assumption

Most significant errors in expected return work do not come from arithmetic. They come from a single embedded assumption that is never stated: that the structural conditions underlying the forecast will remain roughly stable.

Margins are assumed to mean-revert gently when competitive or regulatory pressure may alter them more sharply. Diversification relationships are assumed to remain supportive because they recently have been. A strategy with smooth realized history is assumed to continue smoothly because its hidden tail risk has not yet been expressed. In all these cases the investor is importing a view about structural continuity that has not been examined as an assumption.

The corporate profit margin chart illustrates one version of this problem. U.S. corporate profits after tax as a share of GDP expanded substantially from the early 2000s through 2021, reaching historically elevated levels. Many equity return forecasts during that period treated the elevated margin as a durable feature of the competitive landscape rather than as a cyclical high. When margins compressed in 2022, alongside a sharp rise in discount rates, equity valuations proved more sensitive to the combined effect than most forecasts had embedded.

The improvement that matters most in expected return work is not more elaborate modeling. It is more honest accounting of which assumptions are carrying the forecast and what their failure would imply for the stated return. That standard applies directly to the portfolio that section 7 evaluates: the question at every stage is not only what the expected return is but what it depends on — and whether that dependency is consciously accepted or inherited from a projection that has not been examined carefully enough.
